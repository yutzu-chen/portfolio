Case Study 04

User research: choosing the right method at the right product stage

I used different research methods across the product lifecycle: exploratory interviews, post-launch product research, concept validation, questionnaires, and usability testing. The goal was always the same: help the team make better product decisions with real user insights.

Overview

User research is not one single activity. The right method depends on what the team needs to learn and where the product is in its development process.

I used research across different product moments: before planning, after launch, before development, before final design decisions, and before release.

The goal was to support better product decisions with real user insight.

Problem

Sometimes the goal is to understand users before a product direction is clear. Sometimes it is to explain why the current product is not working as expected. Other times, the team already has a concept or design and needs to validate whether it makes sense to users.

It's important to choose the right research method for the right question. A method that works for early discovery is not always the right one for comparing final designs or testing whether users can complete a task.

Solution

I matched the research method to the product decision the team needed to make. That made each study more practical and helped the team move from broad uncertainty to clearer product direction.

01

Explore the problem space

Use interviews when the team needs to understand users, behaviors, needs, and barriers before defining a product direction.

02

Explain current behavior

Use post-launch research to understand why analytics show drop-off, low usage, or unexpected behavior.

03

Validate concepts and usability

Use wireframes, questionnaires, prototypes, and task-based testing before investing more engineering effort.

Research methods

1. Exploratory research

Exploratory research is useful when the team knows the business goal, but does not yet fully understand the users, their behaviors, or the problem space.

This method helps answer questions like:

  • Who are the users?
  • What are their current habits?
  • What problems do they already experience?

Example: Understanding digital receipt behavior

Before planning a digital receipt product, we needed to understand how people in Taiwan managed receipts and why many people still did not use digital receipts.

The research included both users and non-users of digital receipts. That mattered because we wanted to understand current adoption and the barriers that stopped people from switching.

The outcome was a clearer understanding of user personas, current behaviors, barriers to adoption, and possible opportunities for product-market fit.

2. Understanding the current product

After a product is launched, user research can help explain what usage data alone cannot answer. Analytics can show where users drop off or which feature has low usage, but it often does not explain why.

This method helps answer questions like:

  • How do users actually use the product?
  • What do they like or dislike?
  • Why are some features not being used?

Example: Investigating product usage after launch

After the digital receipt product was launched, we wanted to understand how users used the product and how they felt about it. We also saw unusual patterns in the usage data, such as significant drop-offs in the sign-up flow, and interviews helped explain what users experienced during those steps.

The outcome was a better view of product strengths, pain points, and improvement opportunities that were not obvious from data alone.

3. Product concept validation through interviews

Concept validation is useful when the team has a rough product direction, but wants to check whether the idea solves a real user problem before investing too much engineering effort.

At this stage, the product does not need to be fully built. Wireframes, user flows, or low-fidelity prototypes are enough.

This method helps answer questions like:

  • Does the concept solve a real user need?
  • Do users understand the idea?
  • Which features should be prioritized?

Example: Validating new feature ideas with wireframes

After forming a rough product improvement plan, we tested several low-fidelity feature concepts with users. Instead of directly building the features, we showed wireframes and user flows first so feedback could arrive early, when changes were still cheap and easy to make.

The outcome was a clearer view of the strengths and weaknesses of each concept, and better prioritization of which improvements should move forward.

4. Product concept validation through questionnaires

Questionnaires are useful when the team wants to compare design options with a larger user group. Interviews are better for understanding why users think or behave a certain way. Questionnaires are better when the team needs broader feedback on preference, clarity, or perceived usefulness.

This method helps answer questions like:

  • Which design do users prefer?
  • Which concept feels more useful?

Example: Comparing high-fidelity design options

Before developing new features, we prepared several high-fidelity UI options and tested them through a questionnaire. Users were shown different design versions and asked which one they preferred, what they thought about the feature, and in what situations they would use it.

The outcome was broader comparison data that helped the team make a more confident design decision.

5. Usability testing

Usability testing is useful when the team wants to see whether users can complete specific tasks with a design or prototype. Instead of asking users what they think in general, we observe what they actually do.

This method helps answer questions like:

  • Can users complete the task?
  • Where do they get stuck?
  • Which parts of the UI are unclear?

Example: Testing a redesigned app flow

In a university UX project, we redesigned part of a convenience store app and tested whether users could complete key tasks. During the test, users were asked to complete several tasks without instruction and to think aloud while using the prototype.

You are at a convenience store and want to collect points for your purchase. You need to show the barcode from the app at the checkout counter. What would you do?

The outcome was a clearer view of usability issues, their frequency and severity, and the improvements needed before moving forward.

My research process

01

Define the research goal

I first clarified what decision the research needed to support. That kept the work focused on product choices rather than generic feedback collection.

02

Choose the right method

I selected the method based on the product stage and the kind of question we needed to answer: exploration, explanation, validation, comparison, or usability.

03

Recruit the right participants

The participant mix changed with the goal: users and non-users for exploratory work, current users for post-launch research, and target users for new concept validation.

04

Prepare materials and run the research

Depending on the project, this meant screening surveys, interview scripts, wireframes, prototypes, questionnaires, and structured note-taking templates.

05

Analyze patterns and pain points

I looked for recurring needs, confusion, barriers, and differences between user groups so the team could see what mattered most.

06

Turn findings into product decisions

The final step was to help the team decide what to build, improve, prioritize, or stop based on what we had learned.

Impact

  • 50+ user interviews across product questions
  • Helped teams choose the right research method for each product stage
  • Explained user behavior that analytics alone could not answer
  • Validated product concepts before heavier design and engineering investment
  • Turned user insights into clearer product decisions and priorities

Reflection

This work taught me that user research is most useful when it is connected to a product decision.

I do not see research as a separate activity that happens only at the beginning of a project. It can support different stages of product development: before planning, after launch, before development, before final design decisions, and before release.

For me, good user research is practical. It helps the team move from “we think” to “we understand this better now,” and then toward a clearer product decision.
